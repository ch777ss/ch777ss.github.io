Emotion as Information

2026-02-07

I used to believe that emotional stability was a virtue.
That clarity meant calmness.
That being “positive” was a form of strength.

Recently, under sustained fatigue, lack of exercise, and a chaotic environment,
I noticed something different.

Emotions did not weaken my thinking.
They sharpened my perception.

From an evolutionary perspective, emotion is tightly coupled with safety.
Tension, anger, anxiety are not moral failures —
they are signals that prediction has broken down.

To feel uneasy is to detect danger.
To feel irritated is to sense misalignment.
To feel overwhelmed is to realize that an explanation no longer holds.

The problem begins when emotions are treated as noise.
When “optimism” is used to suppress signals rather than interpret them.
When calmness is mistaken for understanding.

In Deutsch’s sense, progress does not come from emotional regulation,
but from better explanations.

Emotion is often the first indicator that our current model of reality is insufficient.
Not something to be erased,
but something to be examined.

The task is not to escape chaos,
but to observe within it.

To separate signal from distortion.
To extract facts from feeling.
To gain a more precise understanding of oneself and the environment.

Errors are not endpoints.
They are invitations to improve explanations.

And in that sense, having emotions — and noticing them — is not a regression.
It is the beginning of understanding.
